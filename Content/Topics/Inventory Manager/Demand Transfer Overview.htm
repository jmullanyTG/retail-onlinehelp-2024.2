Demand Transfer Overview

A challenge for omni-channel retailers is ensuring that their replenishment strategies keep up with the options shoppers now have when it comes to fulfilling their purchases. These options include:

  • Buy Online, Pickup In Store (BOPIS)
  • Buy Online, Ship to Home
  • Buy In Store, Ship to Home
  • Buy In Store, Pickup in a Different Store

Each option results in inventory potentially leaving a different site than where it was sold. When this happens, the demand for items is transferred from the sales location to the fulfillment location.

If a replenishment strategy does not account for these demand transfers, negative outcomes (such as overstocks, understocks, lost sales, and costly inventory re-orders and redistributions) can occur.

To help avoid these outcomes, the demand transfer feature offers the option to choose whether items use fulfillment-driven replenishment strategies or the traditional sales-driven replenishment strategy.

Demand transfer from a DC to a store is not supported.

Sales-driven replenishment

In a traditional retail scenario, sales and fulfillment are assumed to happen at the same location. A typical example would be a customer walking into a store, taking an item off of the shelf, purchasing it at the register, and taking it with them as they walk out of the store.

In such scenarios:

  • demand for an item remains at its sale location.
  • future replenishment orders for a store are based on that store's forecasted sales demand.

What is sales demand?

Sales demand is a type of forecast that predicts future demand for items at a location based on historical sales at that location.

Different types of sales demand forecasts can be created, including:

  • inventory, level, group, and shape forecasts generated by Demand Forecasting.
  • profiles generated by Profiles.
  • rates of sale generated by Rate of Sale.
  • allocations forecasts generated by Allocation.

Fulfillment-driven replenishment

In omni-channel scenarios, sales and fulfillment can happen at different locations.

For example, in a BOPIS scenario, a shopper buys something online and then gets to choose the brick-and-mortar store where they will pick up their purchases.

In these scenarios:

  • demand for an item is transferred from the sales location to the fulfillment location.
  • future replenishment orders for a store should be based on that store's forecasted fulfillment demand.

What is fulfillment demand?

Fulfillment demand is a type of forecast that predicts future demand for items at a location based on historic fulfillment at that location.

The following fulfillment demand forecasts can be generated:

  • Split factors
  • Split factor forecasts (for more information, refer to the Inventory Manager User Guide)

Choosing an item's replenishment strategy

By default, ToolsGroup JustEnough uses a location's projected sales demand to replenish orders at a location. Fulfillment demand can be used instead on an item-by-item basis.

 For best results, use the same replenishment strategy for all locations where an item is sold.
