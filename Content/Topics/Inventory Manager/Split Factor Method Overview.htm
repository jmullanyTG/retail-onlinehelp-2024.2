Split factors

Split factors are a way of calculating fulfillment demand.

A split factor is the percentage of a location’s historical sales that were fulfilled at a given location. For example, if Store A fulfilled 100% of its past sales (meaning all items bought from Store A were fulfilled by Store A), the split factor for Store A sales would be Store A = 100%.

If Store A only fulfilled 90% of its past sales and the remaining 10% of sales was fulfilled at Store B, the split factors for Store A's sales would be: Store A = 90%, Store B = 10%.

Split factors must be calculated for all locations in which an item is sold. This gives the application a complete history of where sales at each location were fulfilled. Captured as split factors, this fulfillment history can then be applied to each location's sales forecasts. The result is a forecast for future fulfillment demand at each location.

In Store A's case, if 10% of its sales are expected to be fulfilled by Store B, then replenishment orders for both stores need to be modified accordingly. Otherwise, Store A may end up being overstocked while Store B may experience unexpected stock outs after fulfilling demand from its own sales as well as demand transferred from Store A sales.

Calculating split factors

The calculation used to determine a split factor is:

# of Units sold at [Sales Location] that were fulfilled at [Fulfillment Location] / # of Units sold at [Sales Location]

Example: Buy Online, Pickup In Store (BOPIS)

An e-commerce site gives customers the choice of picking up items from their local store or having the item shipped to their home.

In such a scenario, all sales are credited to the e-commerce site, but fulfillment of sales is split among multiple locations based on customer choices. If a customer chooses to pick up the item in a store, fulfillment is transferred to the pickup store. If they choose to ship it to their home, fulfilment stays at the e-commerce location.

Once split factors have been calculated from sales at all locations (e-commerce, Store 73, Store 241, Store 405), they are used to forecast fulfillment demand for each location.

Applying split factors

To calculate how much stock on hand is needed to handle a location’s anticipated demand, the system applies the split factors calculated from each location’s sales history to each location’s sales demand forecast.

For example, a sales forecast is created for the next 90 days (Period 2) based on sales from the previous 90 days (Period 1).

To then calculate anticipated fulfillment demand at each location, the system:

  • calculates split factors for each location based on Period 1 fulfillment splits (see Calculating split factors).
  • applies the Period 1 split factors to the Period 2 sales forecasts (see examples below).
  • sums up the split factor results for each location from across all locations (see examples below).

Enabling split factors in replenishment strategies

Split factors can be enabled when calculating an item’s replenishment strategy. For more information, see Setting the Consider Demand Transference option for an item.
